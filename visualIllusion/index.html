Does an inverted shadow still provide motion cues?

Jake Chuharski, Nick Dow, Preston Hess

In class, we saw that dark shadows provide strong depth cues. This was shown next to an image with inverted shadows, which were white. The objects looked like they had some white spray paint behind them, which did not provide depth cues. We also saw an illusion where a sqaure was still, but had a shadow behind it that moved at an angle. This made the sqaure appear to pop out in depth, and caused us to perceive motion. We saw a similar effect with a ball that moved on a consistent path, but had a shadow that moved in a different direction. This made the ball appear to move in a different path, either rolling or gaining height and dropping back down in an arc.

With our Illusion, we aim to explore the intersection of these effects. We will have a ball that moves in a consistent path, but rather than providing depth cues with a shadow, we will provide an inverted shadow. This type of shadow did not provide depth cues for the stationary objects, but may still provide depth cues for moving objects. We hypothesize that the ball will appear to take a different path depending on the inverted shadow provided with it.
